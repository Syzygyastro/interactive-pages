What is RIIO?

The framework Ofgem uses to set what monopoly network companies are allowed to earn — and what they have to deliver in return.

RIIO stands for Revenue = Incentives + Innovation + Outputs. Each price-control period (5 years for the T2/T3/ED2/ED3 cycle) Ofgem agrees with each licensee a totex allowance, a set of outputs the licensee must deliver, and a package of incentives (financial and reputational) that flex revenue up or down based on performance. There are four live controls today:

  • RIIO-T2 (2021 – 2026) — just closed; final reconciliation in flight.
  • RIIO-T3 (2026 – 2031) — just started 1 April 2026.
  • RIIO-ED2 (2023 – 2028) — mid-control; Year 4 of 5, ~62 % through delivery.
  • RIIO-ED3 (2028 – 2033) — methodology stage; SSMD due 2026.
Why this matters to Grid Agents Every connection process, study workflow, and innovation budget the team meets in the field is shaped by the active RIIO control on either side of the meter. T3 just started and ED3 is being written this year — this is the window where regulatory framing for AI-driven planning, automated curtailment, and anticipatory investment is actually decided.

RIIO-T2 vs RIIO-T3 — transmission price controls

T2 just closed (March 2026); T3 is now live. Ofgem's Final Determinations for T3 land the largest single allowance step-change in network history, driven by ASTI delivery flowing into baseline, the SSEP/CSNP pipeline, and post-CMP434/435 connection-reform investment.

ASTI — the £20 bn sidecar

Accelerated Strategic Transmission Investment is the bespoke regulatory regime Ofgem ran in parallel with T2 to fast-track 26 large network projects (HVDC links, onshore reinforcement) needed by 2030.

  • Fixed Output Delivery Incentive (FOI) — financial reward for hitting in-service dates and penalties for slippage.
  • Fixed Cost Incentive (FCI) — fixed cap on costs with limited reopeners; TO bears overrun risk above the cap.
  • Streamlined consenting pathway alongside DESNZ and Planning Inspectorate.
  • ASTI delivery rolls into T3 baseline allowance for ongoing operation and incentive treatment.

RIIO-ED2 · Distribution
Ofgem is Britain's energy regulator. Every five years it sets how much each Distribution Network Operator (DNO) — the company that owns the wires from the local substation to your house — is allowed to earn from electricity bills. The control today is called RIIO-ED2 (2023 – 2028). Roughly half of a DNO's revenue is fixed in advance. The other half flexes up or down on performance: bonuses for things customers and Ofgem care about, fines for failing them. That flex is where Grid Agents earns money for the network.
Realistic annual swing · one DNO group
£8 – 12 m / year
From performance bonuses captured plus penalties avoided. Across the 14 GB distribution licences (in 6 ownership groups), the prize is materially larger.

↑ Rewards captured

Customer Satisfaction (CSS)
up to ~£8 m / yr
Ofgem hires an independent firm to survey customers after a connection, fault or enquiry on a 1–10 scale. Score above target → revenue bonus. UK Power Networks earned £8.09 m in Year 1 of ED2 — its single biggest performance bonus. Source: Ofgem RIIO-2 Electricity Distribution Annual Report 2023-24.
Time to Connect (TTC)
~£1 – 2 m / yr per group
Working days from a valid connection application to power flowing. Beat the DNO-specific target → bonus. Across the industry, DNOs earned £15.45 m combined in the final ED1 year (2022-23). Source: Ofgem ED1 closeout reporting; NGED "Your Power Future" public dashboard.
Interruption performance (IIS)
~£10 – 20 m / yr per group
Tracks customer minutes lost to power cuts. Across the 8-year ED1 control DNOs earned ~£647 m in total — about £123 m / yr industry-wide. Volatile: ED2 targets are tighter and the upside is harder to reach. Source: Ofgem ED1 closeout reporting.
Totex retained share
~£3 – 5 m / yr per group
Of every £1 the DNO spends below allowance, it keeps about 50p; the rest goes back to consumers in cheaper bills. The £22.2 bn ED2 allowance is ~£4.4 bn / year industry-wide. A 2 % efficiency saves ~£44 m, of which ~£22 m is retained, ÷ 6 ownership groups ≈ £3.7 m / yr each. Source: RIIO-ED2 Final Determinations, Ofgem 30 Nov 2022.

↓ Penalties avoided

Interruption (IIS) penalty
£m-scale per group
The downside of the IIS metric above. UK Power Networks took a £5.90 m penalty in Year 1 of ED2 as targets re-baselined. Faster fault diagnosis, smarter restoration sequencing and automated customer comms during incidents directly reduce exposure. Source: Ofgem RIIO-2 ED Annual Report 2023-24.
Major Connections (MCI)
cap ~0.9 % of base revenue
Survey-based score from large connection customers (industrial sites, developers). Below threshold → penalty. SSE Southern took £0.74 m in Year 1; Ofgem has signalled tighter thresholds going forward. Source: Ofgem RIIO-2 ED Annual Report 2023-24.
GSOP per-customer payouts
automatic
Guaranteed Standards of Performance are statutory deadlines: typically 45 working days for a low-voltage connection offer, 65 for high-voltage. Miss them and the DNO pays an automatic, fixed amount per affected customer. Pre-screened applications and faster design turnaround keep the clock under control. Source: Electricity (Standards of Performance) Regulations; Ofgem GSOP guidance.
Complaint penalties
small £m, rising
Volume of complaints normalised by customer base. ED2 makes this penalty-only — no upside. Year 1 industry totals stayed modest but trended upward as connection volumes surged. Source: Ofgem RIIO-2 ED Annual Report 2023-24.
Worked example — totex retained share Imagine Ofgem sets a corner-shop owner's grocery budget at £400 / month. Spend £380 and you keep £10 of the £20 saved — the rest goes back to customers as cheaper prices. Now scale that to a DNO group: the £22.2 bn ED2 allowance is the budget; about £4.4 bn / year flows industry-wide. A 2 % efficiency from less manual rework, fewer truck rolls and faster design hand-offs saves ~£44 m. Half flows back as retained revenue. Across 6 ownership groups, that's ≈ £3.7 m / yr each — on top of the ODI-F bonuses above.

RIIO-T3 · Transmission & ASTI
Ofgem also regulates the three Transmission Owners (TOs) — National Grid Electricity Transmission, SSEN Transmission, and SP Transmission — that own the high-voltage long-distance network across Great Britain, plus NESO, the system operator. The control today is called RIIO-T3 (2026 – 2031). Alongside it sits ASTI: a ~£20 bn programme of 26 strategic projects (HVDC subsea links, onshore reinforcement) being fast-tracked for delivery by 2030. On both, much of the TO's profit moves with delivery and project performance. That movement is where Grid Agents earns money for the TO.
Per-project & per-year £ at stake · one TO
single-digit to high-£tens of millions
Per ASTI project on schedule and cost performance, plus annual £m-scale flex on system-performance metrics — set against a £10.7 bn T3 upfront baseline within a ~£90 bn whole-RIIO-3 envelope.

↑ Rewards captured

Energy Not Supplied (ENS) reward
£m / yr per TO
Tracks how much electricity wasn't delivered to customers because of TO outages, weighted by megawatt-hours. Beat target → bonus; miss it → fine. Reported per-TO each year. Predictive asset-health and contingency-planning agents reduce avoidable outages and shorten restoration time. Source: Ofgem RIIO-2 Electricity Transmission Annual Reports 2023-24, 2024-25.
Totex retained share (T3 stepped TIM)
band-dependent
Spend below allowance → keep a banded share of the saving. ED2's flat ~50 % becomes a band-by-band split under T3, with GT3 (gas) at 39 % retained. The cap on upside means smarter allocation of effort — putting AI-led design automation where the band still pays well — matters more in T3 than it did in T2. Source: RIIO-3 Final Determinations, Ofgem 4 Dec 2025.
Innovation funding (NIA + SIF)
£450 m programme
Off-totex pots that pay for partnered pilot work. Ofgem and UKRI have £450 m allocated to the Strategic Innovation Fund through 2028, plus per-licensee Network Innovation Allowance (typical project £100 k – £2 m, partners keep IP). The route by which early Grid Agents work gets paid for without touching the TO's totex. Source: Ofgem / UKRI SIF programme; RIIO-2 NIA decisions.

↓ Penalties avoided

ASTI Fixed Output Incentive (FOI)
multi-£m per project
Each of the 26 ASTI projects (~£20 bn portfolio) carries a per-project bonus for hitting the in-service date and a per-project penalty for slippage. Schedules slip mostly on the soft side — consenting, third-party engagement, design version control, change-order review — exactly where AI-assisted document workflows compress time. Source: Ofgem ASTI Guidance & Submission Requirements, Aug 2023.
ASTI Fixed Cost Incentive (FCI)
£tens – £hundreds m cap per project
Each ASTI project has a fixed cost cap with limited reopeners. Every £ spent above the cap comes off the TO's own profit. AI-assisted scope control, change-order analysis and supply-chain risk monitoring directly compress overrun risk. Source: Ofgem ASTI framework decision, Dec 2022.
PCD claw-back
£m per programme
Price Control Deliverables are funded outputs the TO must deliver (cyber uplifts, asset-health programmes, network-output-measures targets). Took the money, didn't deliver → return it. Better project tracking and forecasting reduces hand-back risk. Source: RIIO-T2 Final Determinations Dec 2020; T3 FDs Dec 2025.
Energy Not Supplied (ENS) penalty
£m / yr per TO
The downside of the ENS reward above. T3 maintains the incentive with refined targets and a tighter SF6-emissions trajectory alongside it. Avoidable outages are the line item to drive down. Source: RIIO-3 Final Determinations, 4 Dec 2025.
Worked example — one ASTI project Take a single £1 bn onshore reinforcement scheme. The Fixed Output Incentive offers a per-project bonus for hitting the in-service date and an equivalent penalty for slippage — typically £m to tens of £m per project. The Fixed Cost Incentive caps cost; every £ above the cap comes off TO profit. The schedule risk is overwhelmingly soft: consenting, stakeholder engagement, change-order review, design version control. Compress those by even a few weeks and the swing tips the right way. Multiplied across the 26-project, ~£20 bn ASTI portfolio, the £ at stake is in the low hundreds of millions across the TO group.
