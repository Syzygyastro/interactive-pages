# Grid Interconnection — Weekly Meeting Notes

The process splits into two routes: transmission for larger projects at 132 kV and above, and distribution for everything below that.

On the transmission side, the developer starts with a pre-application discussion with NESO to understand where the project might connect and whether the network has capacity. The background documents at this point are the CUSC, which is the commercial rulebook that defines how applications are made and offers are structured, and the Grid Code, which is the technical rulebook setting out what data the developer must provide and what technical standards the plant must eventually meet.

The developer then submits a formal application through the NESO portal. At this stage the Electricity Act 1989 matters — it is the legal foundation that gives parties the right to request a connection (do you have the land right, etc.) — and the TMO4+ decision comes in, which is the Ofgem-approved reform that replaced the old first-come-first-served queue with the current gate-based process.

The next step is the gate assessment under TMO4+, where NESO checks whether the project is ready and strategically needed. Two methodologies(documents) govern this: G2CM, which defines what evidence a project must show to prove readiness, and CNDM, which explains how NESO designs the network solution behind each offer (It is the playbook NESO uses to decide what grid upgrades are needed when someone applies for a connection. Every connection offer is computed using this methodology.). There is another document NETS SQSS also relevant here — it is the transmission planning standard that tells NESO how robust the network must be, which drives whether reinforcements are needed.

Once the assessment is complete, NESO issues either a Gate 1 indicative offer or a more meaningful Gate 2 offer with actual connection timing and costs. The developer then signs a Bilateral Connection Agreement with NESO and a Construction Agreement with the relevant Transmission Owner — NGET, SPT, or SHET depending on location. The STC document, which governs how NESO and the Transmission Owner coordinate planning, outages, and delivery, becomes important from this point on.

Construction follows, with the Transmission Owner building the grid-side works and the developer building their plant. As the project nears completion, the developer goes through Grid Code compliance testing — proving the plant can meet fault ride-through, reactive power, and frequency response requirements. ESQCR 2002, the statutory electrical safety regulation covering clearances and earthing, applies throughout construction and testing. Finally NESO issues operational notifications, the Transmission Owner does the physical switching, and the project is energised.

On the distribution side, the DNO leads. Very small systems use G98, a fit-and-inform route where the installer puts the system in and notifies the DNO afterwards. Larger projects go through G99 — the developer makes a feasibility enquiry to the DNO, then submits a formal application backed by the G99 engineering recommendation, which sets the technical requirements for embedded generation on distribution networks. The DNO assesses the network using the D-Code, which is the technical rulebook for distribution, and the DCUSA, which is the commercial and charging framework for distribution connections. ESQCR and the Electricity Act apply here too. The DNO then issues a connection offer — sometimes a flexible connection in constrained areas, meaning the project connects sooner but can be curtailed when the network is stressed. After acceptance it is construction, commissioning, and energisation.

So across both routes, the documents are answering the same questions at each stage: who has the right to connect, who runs the process, how is the network assessed, what technical standards apply, and how is the connection finally delivered.
